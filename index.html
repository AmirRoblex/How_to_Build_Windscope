Build Your Own Interactive Wind Park Simulator!

Welcome, Future Engineers!

Have you ever looked at a giant wind turbine and wondered how it works? Or how a whole field of them—a wind park—is planned? The switch to renewable energy is one of the most important challenges of our time, and wind power is a huge part of the solution.

With Windscope, you get to be the planner! You'll build your own interactive model of a wind park. Using special blocks, you'll design a landscape, place wind turbines, and see a real-time simulation of your park's energy output. You can even control the wind with physical knobs!

The finished Windscope exhibit in action. (Photo: Maximilian Reiner)

What You Will Learn:

  • Physics: Understand how wind speed and direction affect energy generation and create a "wind shadow" or wake effect.
  • Engineering: Learn about the physical structure and assembly of an interactive exhibit, from the wooden frame to the electronic components.
  • Renewable Energy: Discover the real-world challenges of wind park planning, like turbine placement, distance rules, and maximizing efficiency.
  • Computer Science: Get a peek into how a camera, a computer, and a projector work together to create a seamless, real-time simulation.

Assembling Your Windscope

1. Interactive Table Setup

Construct a transparent-surface table (120x85 cm) with a support arm for mounting. Mount a camera underneath and a projector overhead on the support arm. Hide the connected Intel NUC10i7FNH Mini PC underneath the table. Add a touch screen interface (iiyama ProLite TF1634MC-B8X). Mount a second webcam on top to capture surface interaction. Connect all devices to the PC: camera, projector, touchscreen, and webcams.

2. Transparent LEGO Grid Board

Inside the top, create a ledge for the 56x56cm plexiglass sheet to sit on. A CNC-milled grid on the plexiglass is highly recommended for perfect alignment. Ensure 1.75 mm depth per tile square. Use provided grid spacing and drilling specs for precision.

3. LEGO City Tiles

Build tiles for each city zone: residential, commercial, industrial, roads, parks, water. You need to build 256 blocks. The most important part is getting the black and white pattern on the bottom correct for each type, as this is how the camera tells them apart. To build each building, follow each of the instructions that you can find in the following URL: LEGO Building Instructions

Refer to this pattern guide when building your blocks. Accuracy is key!

Calibration is performed in two separate steps:

3.1 Initial Camera Calibration (Lens Distortion Removal)

Performed once when the camera is permanently fixed to the model’s base plate. This removes fish-eye distortion. Follow the OpenCV tutorial: OpenCV tutorial and save the generated camera matrix and distortion coefficients.

3.2 Grid Edge Calibration (calibrate.py)

Defines the grid boundaries within the scanner software.

$ python calibrate.py

Click the corners in this order: top left, bottom left, top right, bottom right. Repeat if the plate or camera is moved.

Let's Go! Running the Project

You've built the hardware and installed the software. It's time to bring your Windscope to life!

5. Running the Software

Three processes must run at the same time:

5.1 Start the UI (http-server)

From the `windpark-simulator` root:

$ npm run build

$ http-server -c-1

This serves the built UI. Note the link displayed in the terminal; it is needed for accessing the dashboard and city view.

5.2 Start the Backend Server

From `windpark-simulator/server`:

$ npm start

This launches the Node.js backend that processes scanner data and updates the simulation.

5.3 Start the Scanner

From the `scanner` directory:

$ python run.py

A live camera feed should appear with detected tiles outlined.

6. Accessing the Interfaces

The simulation is now running! Open a web browser (like Firefox) on the Mini-PC.

  • Dashboard: Open the copied `http-server` link in Firefox and append `/dashboard` (e.g., `http://:8081/dashboard`). Press F11 for full screen.
  • City View: Open another Firefox window with the same base link but append `/city`. Press F11 and move the window to the projector display.

If the city projection does not align with the physical grid, adjust `max-width` and `max-height` in the browser inspector until it fits.

7. Notes

  • The windspeed and direction knobs only function if the City View window is active.
  • Return knobs to starting positions before running simulations.
  • Repeat calibration steps if tile detection is inaccurate.

How to Play & Learn the Science

Experiment by placing blocks and turning the wind knobs. Watch how the dashboard changes in real-time. Here's the science behind what you're seeing:

The Power Curve: Wind Speed is Key!

Not all wind is the same. Wind turbines have a "sweet spot" for generating power. Use your wind speed knob and watch the Power (Leistung) on the dashboard.

  • Too Slow: Below the "cut-in speed" (Anlaufgeschwindigkeit, ~3 m/s), the blades won't even turn. No power!
  • Just Right: As speed increases, power skyrockets! It increases with the CUBE of the velocity.
  • Rated Speed: At a certain point (~14 m/s), the turbine reaches its maximum "rated power" (Nennleistung). Even if the wind gets faster, it won't produce more power, to protect itself.

The Wake Effect: Don't Block the Wind!

When a turbine captures wind, it leaves a "wind shadow" of slower, more turbulent air behind it. This is called the wake effect (Nachlauf). Placing another turbine in this wake is a bad idea!

  • Less Power: The downstream turbine gets less "fuel" (wind) and produces much less electricity.
  • Shorter Lifespan: The turbulent, choppy air causes extra wear and tear (Materialermüdung), reducing the turbine's lifespan. Watch the Lifespan (Lebensdauer) indicator on your dashboard!

Try it! Place two turbines in a row and turn the wind direction knob so one is directly behind the other. See what happens to the Power and Lifespan values.

The wake of slower, turbulent air behind a turbine.

Distance Rules (Abstandsregelungen)

In the real world, there are strict rules about where you can build turbines to protect people and nature. The simulator includes these! If you place a turbine too close to something, a red indicator will appear.

  • To Houses: Turbines must be a safe distance from settlements to protect residents from noise and "shadow flicker."
  • To Each Other: To avoid the wake effect, turbines need to be spaced out—typically 5-7 times their rotor diameter apart in the main wind direction!
  • To Water/Roads: You also need to respect buffer zones around infrastructure and natural features.

The Science Lab: A Technical Deep Dive

For the curious minds who want to know *exactly* how it works. Let's look under the hood at the science and technology powering Windscope.

The Jensen Wake Model: Simulating Wind Shadows

The "wake effect" isn't random; it's calculated using a simplified physics model developed by N.O. Jensen. The model assumes the wind shadow expands linearly behind the turbine. The simulation uses this formula to calculate the reduced wind speed ($v_1$) at a distance ($x$) behind a turbine:

v₁(x) = v₀ * (1 - (2*a) / (1 + (2*k*x)/D)² )

In the simulation, this formula is pre-calculated for different distances, which determines the percentage of power loss for downstream turbines.

Teacher's Hub: Using Windscope in the Classroom

Windscope is more than just a model; it's a dynamic sandbox for exploring complex topics in a tangible way. Here’s how you can leverage it for your classroom.

Learning Objectives

By the end of a session with Windscope, students should be able to:

  • Explain the relationship between wind speed and power generation (the power curve).
  • Define the "wake effect" and explain why it's a critical factor in wind park design.
  • Identify the social and environmental factors (like distance rules) that influence wind park planning.
  • Collaborate in a team to design a solution that balances competing goals (e.g., maximizing power vs. protecting turbine lifespan).

Teaching Physics & Science with Windscope

Use the exhibit to make abstract science concepts tangible and interactive.

  • Physics: Visually demonstrate the transformation of kinetic energy (wind) into electrical energy. The "Power" value on the dashboard is a direct measurement. Use the power curve to discuss concepts of work, power, and efficiency.
  • Geography & Environmental Science: The exhibit is a perfect tool for discussing land use conflicts. Why can't we build a wind park in a residential area or a nature reserve? Students must make decisions about site selection that balance energy needs with social and environmental protection.
  • Computer Science & Engineering: Discuss the system as an example of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI). How do the physical blocks (input) get translated into a digital simulation (output)? This is a great introduction to modeling, simulation, and the Internet of Things (IoT).

Guiding Questions to Spark Discussion

Use these questions to encourage critical thinking while students are using the exhibit:

  • "What happens to the total power when you cluster all the turbines close together vs. spreading them out? Why do you think that is?"
  • "If the wind is coming from the north, is it better to arrange your turbines in a horizontal line or a vertical line? Why?"
  • "Find the wind speed that gives you the most power. What happens if you go much faster than that? Is more wind always better?"
  • "Look at the 'Lifespan' indicator. What actions make it go down? How can you design a park that is both powerful and long-lasting?"
  • "Why do you think there are rules about how close turbines can be to houses? What problems might they cause in real life?"

5-Day Windscope Classroom Challenge

Here is a sample week-long plan to structure learning around the Windscope project. This can be adapted for different age groups and timeframes.

Day 1: The Grand Tour - Introduction & Free Play

Objective: Familiarize students with the exhibit and spark initial curiosity.

  • Activity: Class discussion: "What do we already know about wind energy?"
  • Introduce the Windscope exhibit. Explain the different block types.
  • Free Play: Allow students in small groups to freely explore the exhibit for 15-20 minutes.
  • Wrap-up: Ask groups to share one interesting thing they discovered.

Day 2: The Science Lab - Power & Wakes

Objective: Understand the core physics concepts of the simulation.

  • Activity: Guided exploration of the "How to Play" and "Science Lab" tabs.
  • Challenge 1 (Power Curve): "Your goal is to find the exact wind speed that generates the most power. Record your findings."
  • Challenge 2 (Wake Effect): "Place two turbines in a line. Measure the total power. Now place them side-by-side. Which is better? Why?"

Day 3: The Planner's Desk - Rules & Goals

Objective: Introduce the social and regulatory constraints of wind park planning.

  • Activity: Introduce the "Distance Rules" and the "Goals" on the dashboard. Discuss why these rules exist in the real world.
  • Challenge: The Perfect Park. "Your team's mission is to design a wind park that satisfies all the citizen's goals and makes all the status indicators turn green."

Day 4: The Real World - Local Energy

Objective: Connect the simulation to the students' own environment.

  • Activity: Research a real wind park in Germany. Where is it? How many turbines?
  • Challenge: Design for Our Town. "Using a map of our local area, find the best place to build a new wind park. Use Windscope to model your proposal."

Day 5: The Energy Summit - Presentations

Objective: Synthesize learning and communicate findings.

  • Activity: Each team presents their "Perfect Park" or "Design for Our Town" proposal.
  • They must explain their design choices using concepts like wake effect, power curve, and distance rules.
  • Final Discussion: What are the biggest challenges in switching to renewable energy?

Troubleshooting & FAQ

Running into a problem? Don't worry! Here are some solutions to common issues.

The projector image is blurry or not aligned.

1. Focus: Most projectors have a focus ring around the lens. Adjust it until the image is sharp.

2. Keystone Correction: If the image is trapezoidal, use the projector's keystone settings to make it a perfect rectangle.

3. Alignment: Physically move the projector until the projected grid perfectly overlaps with the physical grid on the plexiglass.

The scanner isn't detecting my blocks correctly.

1. Re-calibrate: The most common issue! Re-run the `python calibrate.py` script. Make sure to show the chessboard pattern at many different angles and distances.

2. Lighting: Ensure the area under the table is evenly lit and there are no strong reflections on the plexiglass.

3. Check Patterns: Double-check that the black and white patterns on the bottom of your blocks exactly match the guide.

The dashboard shows "0 kW" even with turbines on the board.

1. Check Wind Speed: Is the wind speed knob turned up? If the speed is below the "cut-in speed" (~3 m/s), the turbines won't produce power.

2. Server Connection: Make sure both the scanner script and the server script are running in their terminals without any errors.

One of the scripts crashes on startup.

1. Dependencies: Make sure you have installed all the required libraries by running `pip install -r requirements.txt` in the `scanner` directory and `npm install` in the `windpark-simulator` directory.

2. Port Numbers: Ensure no other application on your computer is using port 8000.

3. Ask for Help: If you're still stuck, post a question on the project's GitHub Issues page!
